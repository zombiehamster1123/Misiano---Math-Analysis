Rational Root Theorem

Rational Root Theorem - limits the possible rational roots of a polynomial equation
Given...
$a_0x^n+a_1x^{n-1}+\text" ... "+a_{n-2}x^2+a_{n-1}x+a_n=0$
If $a_0$ & $a_n≠0$, then $\table p=\text"the factors of " a_n; q=\text"the factors of "a_0$
and... $p/q=$ a possible rational root

ex) Solve for all possible rational roots of the equation
$3x^3-5x^2+5x-2=0$
$\table p=±1\text","±2, \text"the possible rational roots are..."; q=±1\text","±3, p/q=±1/1\text","±1/3\text","±2/1\text","±2/3$

Now, determine the actual rational roots
$\table P(x)=3x^3-5x^2+5x-2; \text"only actual root " x=2/3$ $\table P(1)=1; P(1/3)=-.\ov7; P(2)=12; P(2/3)=0$ $\table P(-1)=-15; P(-1/3)=-4.\ov3; P(-2)=-56;P(-2/3)=-8.\ov 4$

If $a_0=1$ & $a_n≠0$, then any rational roots must be integer factors of $a_n$

Descartes' Rule of Signs

tells you the possible # of real zeros (a.k.a. roots, x-intercepts) of a polynomial equation (ignore zero coefficients with this rule)

possible #s of "+" real zeros = # of changes in sign of terms of f(x) or < by an even #
possible #s of "-" real zeros = # of changes in sign of terms of f(-x) or < by an even #

Ex) find the # of real zeros of
$f(x)=x^5-x^4+3x^3+9x^2-x+5$
Possible #s of "+" real zeros = 4, 2, or 0
Possible #s of "-" real zeros = 1

Ex) Determine the + of complex roots, + of real roots, the possible rational roots & the actual rational roots of
$f(x)=x^4+2x^3-9x^2-2x+8$ (there are 4 complex roots)

Descartes' Rule of Signs
